URSA Major

NASA Student Launch

In 2016 through 2017, I worked on URSA Major, a rocket built for the NASA Student Launch Competition. The rocket was to reach as close to a mile in altitude and carry out a scientific challenge. URSA Major was carrying an upright lander that I designed and built with a team.

Payload (Upright Lander) - Design

The goal of the payload was, as the header implies, to land upright. This was accomplished with three self-deploying legs. Under the main parachute at around 1000 feet, a leg is deployed from the payload while it is still connected to the rest of the rocket. The leg, while deploying, pulls out a small parachute. The entire payload section is then released from the rocket, and the remaining two legs are deployed at 900 and 850 feet. These two legs also pull out small parachutes. The three parachutes provide enough stability for the payload to stay vertical, and create enough drag to prevent bouncing on impact. The leg deployment was staged to prevent these parachutes from tangling.

Payload (Upright Lander) - Manufacture

The payload was manufactured in two stages: first, a prototype was made. The legs were 3D printed and installed on a wooden base. The prototype turned out to be harder to build than expected. The base was not properly toleranced when laser cut, grooves needed to be cut out for the legs, and posts needed to be hand cut. After the prototype was flown and subsequently destroyed, the second phase began. The payload was redesigned and toleranced properly. I took note of all the issues we had with the prototype and avoided or solved them in the second revision. For example, the grooves for the arms were now integrated into the baseplate. So were the posts and torsion spring mounts. In fact, the second design process allowed me to see that we had enough space to secure ballast in the baseplate.
